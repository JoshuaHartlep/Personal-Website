# North Carolina Powerlifting State Championships 2026: What Months of Prep Just to Bomb Out Taught Me

### üì± [Watch the TikTok Summary](https://www.tiktok.com/@joshhartlep/video/7604598411467312415)

On January 31st, 2026, I competed at the North Carolina State Championship powerlifting meet just outside of Charlotte. It was my first meet in two years. I had circled that date on my calendar for months.

Technically, I prepared specifically for about eight weeks. Realistically, the journey started way before that.

Before I even began prep, I cut from the 90 kg (198 lb) weight class down to 82.5 kg (182 lb). I wanted to see if I could lean out, stay disciplined, and maintain most of my strength. In my head, it was going to be the best of both worlds.

It was not.

I wasn't on a good program, and although I pushed myself hard every day and stayed displined to the best of my ability, I lost more strength than I expected. Not the end of the world, but definitely noticeable. In hindsight, I think I might have cut the weight too fast. And that forced me to adjust.

---

## Building My Own Prep

I do not have a powerlifting coach. We're all trying to save costs as best we can, and coaches are a pretty penny. Additionally, I wanted to take ownership of the process. So I used ChatGPT as a consultant, read through programming principles, studied RPE-based training, and built my own eight-week program centered entirely around squat, bench, and deadlift.

Some days I trained with friends at Duke. Most days it was just me, headphones on, spreadsheet open, following percentages I wrote myself.

It was strangely isolating, but also empowering. Every lift was intentional. Every week had a purpose.

One of the biggest lessons during prep was that **training smarter beats training harder**.

For years, I equated progress with suffering. If I was not crawling out from under the bar, I did not feel like I was doing enough. But as I researched, I let myself accept what I always _knew_ but didn't want to admit. I stayed in the RPE 6 to 8 range. Leaving one or two reps in the tank. Walking away feeling strong instead of destroyed.

That was hard for me.

But it works.

And it applies to everything. Coursework. Personal projects. Even internships and applications. Going all out every single day sounds noble, but it is rarely optimal. It's hard to really internalize until you go through it yourself, but it really is true: sustainable progress wins.

---

## The Invisible Work

Another thing prep forced me to confront was how much the other 23 hours of the day matter.

Sleep. Hydration. Stress. Food quality. Body weight fluctuations. Even mindset.

You cannot out-train bad recovery.

Some weeks, I felt strong. Other weeks, I felt stuck under the same weights. Progress was not linear at all. And honestly, it still is not. Unforunately, it hasn't been for years and sometimes I get really frusturated from it. But I trust the process more now. Strength builds quietly before it shows up loudly. I learned this in my academic life too. I literally applied to hundreds of internships this fall, spending hours a day on it just to not even get interviews from 99% of the companies. I thought I would never get an offer, just like sometimes I think I'll never hit my lifting goals. But eventually my work all paid off, and now I can't wait to go to Asana in NYC this summer and learn so so much.

---

# Meet Weekend

We drove down the day before. Three students from Duke came with me, two of which I hadn't met before. Being in a new environment, cutting weight, thinking about attempts, meeting new people, it is a lot mentally.

The morning of the meet, I was extremely nervous. Watching my new friends compete and kill it in the earlier session made it worse. I kept thinking about how strong I used to be at 198. Numbers that used to move fast now felt heavier at 182.

That comparison was unnecessary.

I should have focused on the present version of myself, but it was just too hard to do.

## Bench Press: The Reality Check

Bench is where everything fell apart.

For the last couple of years, my butt has consistently come off the bench when I press heavy. In USAPL, that is not allowed. Glutes must stay down. Heels must stay flat.

I always told myself I would fix it before meet day.

I never fully did.

In powerlifting, your first attempt (opener) should be extremely easy. I set my opener at 110 kg (242.5 lbs). That was ego. At 198, 240 felt easy. I convinced myself I should still be able to hit it easy.

During warmups, I felt slow. After some deliberation, I finally came down to earth and tried to lower my opener to 235 at the table. Unfortunately, the guy told me it was too late. Once the round is about to start, you are locked in.

# The Real Takeaways

### 1. Ego Is Expensive

My opener selection on bench was ego. Ignoring my technical flaw was ego. Believing I could ‚Äújust fix it‚Äù on meet day was ego.

That cost me my total.

In lifting and in life, ego disguises itself as confidence. They are not the same thing.

This was a wakeup call for me, and honestly, I am now happy this happened. I know what I have to do in the future, and this exposed a flaw I have in many aspects of life.

---

### 2. Follow the Process Exactly

If you know something is wrong in training, it will not magically disappear under bright lights. This goes for everything.

From now on, every bench rep in training follows competition standards. Heels flat. Glutes planted. Pause commands respected. No exceptions.

Cutting corners compounds.

When practicing for speeches or events, I can't tell myself after every stutter it was just a one time thing, or I'll clean it up when there's an audience. When doing my homework, I won't look at the answers at the first sign of struggle. I will hold myself to a higher standard in everything I train for, and I believe while it will be harder it will help me grow.

---

### 3. Training Smarter Wins Long Term

RPE 6 to 8 felt too easy at first. But it built the base that allowed me to squat 405 and deadlift 502 even after bombing out.

There is a difference between intensity and recklessness.

I am learning that and trying my best to implement it now.

---

### 4. Do Not Compare to an Old Version of Yourself

I kept thinking about 198-pound me.

But that version of me had a different bodyweight, different leverage, different context.

The only comparison that matters is whether I am better than I was yesterday, because that is all I can really change. Everything else is purely detrimental mindset, and I will work on not getting hung up on things now out of my control.

---

### 5. Resilience Is a Choice

I cried. Then I walked back out and pulled over 500 pounds.

Life is going to hand out red lights sometimes. The question is whether you lay down or you keep lifting.

---

Overall, I am not happy with the outcome. I wish I had not bombed out. I wish I had trusted the process earlier.

But I am proud of how I responded.

This meet did not go how I planned.

The next one will.
